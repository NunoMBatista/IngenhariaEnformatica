This is how we work:
    projection_matrix(4 * 4) * [ x y z w ] = [ a b c d ], w = 1
    From left to right: projection matrix, input, output.

Example of operations:
- Translation
$$
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 3 \\
0 & 1 & 5 \\
0 & 0 & 1 \\
\end{bmatrix}

- Scaling
$$
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 2 & 0 \\

\end{bmatrix}

- Rotation
$$
\begin{bmatrix}
cos(theta) & -sin(theta) & 0 \\
sin(theta) & cos(theta) & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 1 \\
\end{bmatrix}


These last three are examples of 2D projection changes.
In 2D we only have two plains for rotation... In 3D we have 3 plains, so we will actually have more matrizes of rotation... 
One for each axis. Each also has a name: roll, pitch and yaw.

Q: When we render an image, and move it...
    Who is actualy moving?
    The camera? Or the world?
    In theory either one or the other is correct, because we will get the same results with one or the other
    considered as "the origin".

But we will come back to this, becase this notion is important.
In order to move something we must define the movement, then translate this into the projection matrix.
Now  we actually need to calculate the inverse matrix of the projection matrix.
Only then apply the transformation as the operation we saw before:
    projection_matrix(4 * 4) * [ x y z w ] = [ a b c d ], w = 1

Note: (why the inverse?)

My operation becomes:
    P * (View * V)

P - projection matrix (transforms the camera space into clip space. Maps 3D into 2D.)
View - transforms the world space into camera space (view space). It aplies camera position and rotation accoding to object 
we want to 'photograph'.
V - the vertices of our object.

Recall the matricial operation of multiplication:
    A * (B * C) = (A * B) * C
We apply comutation in multplication... If we apply this to our case:

    P * (View * V)
    ...we can trun this calculation into:
    (P * View) * V

Why does this little detail matter?
Because it is much easer to make this calculation: i am only feeding the GPU two small matrices (3*3 or 4*4).
This is much best than having o apply first View to all the vertices... and then multiply P by the result we got before.
I do not have to feed the GPU hunders and hunders of lines, accordign to he vertices i want to project...

    This means: "It's easier to move the worl than the camera"

Let's imagine i have two objects. I want to make their movement fairly independent... That is, i can operate 
each one independently. But if, in the real world, they have some sort of dependency (physical behavior), then
I have to tweek my matrices...
    
    P * V. M1 * x1
    P * V M2 * x2
P -> projection matrixes
V -> view matrix
M ->  mode matrix
v -> vertices (object)

View is unique -> it only concerns the camera itself.
The model matrix, M1 and M2, are specific to the objects, x1 and x2.

Now think about physical behavior, their hierarchy.
Clip: a character jumps onto a carriage of a train. First both are still. But then  carriage starts moving.
If the object were completely independ, and if no additional math consideres, the carriage would go on, 
and the character would fall off from it... But that is not what happens in the real world (or what we want 
in our program...). When the carriage strats moving the character goes along!
Taht means that the movement that is applied in the carriage is also applied to the character.
But that is not all. If the character iself want to move an arm, a leg... Then we also have to apply These
transformations accordingly!

That means we have a cascade, a hierarchy of movement, which translates into cumulative applciation of model matrices:

    train           M(train)
      |
    character       M(train) * M(character)
  /   |   \
leg  arm   body     M(train) * M(character) * M(body)
